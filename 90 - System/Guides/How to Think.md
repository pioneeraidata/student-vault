# 🧭 How to Think

A few mental models that carry across every class and most of life. None of these are about a subject. They are about how to attack a problem. Learn them once and you will use them forever.

## Try the simple thing first

When you have a problem, start with the simplest approach that could work. Only reach for something more complicated when the simple one actually fails to do the job.

People love to overcomplicate. They write the fancy essay structure before they can state their point in one sentence. They jump to the hard method before trying the obvious one. Start simple. If simple works, you are done and you saved an hour. If it does not, now you know exactly why you need more, and you climb one step up, not ten.

## It depends on the problem

There is rarely one right answer that works everywhere. The right move depends on the specific situation in front of you. So before you pick a method, read the actual question. What is it really asking? What do you actually have to work with?

This sounds obvious and almost nobody does it. They pattern-match to the last problem and apply the same move. Slow down for five seconds and let the specific problem tell you what it needs.

## Fix the foundation, not the polish

If the base is bad, no amount of surface work saves it. A shaky argument does not get better with nicer words. A math answer built on a wrong first step does not get better with neater handwriting. Weak sources do not get better with a longer bibliography.

When something is not working, do not polish harder. Go back down and check the foundation. Usually the real problem is three steps earlier than where you are frustrated.

## A first try is a starting point, not the answer

Your first draft, your first attempt, your first result: none of those are the final thing. They are a benchmark to improve from. This takes the pressure off starting. The first version is allowed to be bad, because its only job is to exist so you can make it better. Get something down, then fix it. A rough thing you can improve beats a perfect thing you never started.

## Separate what happened from what to do about it

Three different things, and people mash them together:

- **What happened** (the fact): "I got a 68 on the test."
- **Why** (the interpretation): "I understood the reading but bombed the math section."
- **What to do** (the decision): "Redo every math problem I missed and quiz myself before the next one."

Keep them separate and you think clearly. The fact is not an insult. The interpretation is where the useful information is. The decision is the only part you actually control. This works on a grade, a game, a friendship, anything.

## Correlation is not causation

Two things happening together does not mean one caused the other. Ice cream sales and sunburns both go up in summer. Ice cream does not cause sunburn. The sun causes both.

You will get sold this trick constantly, in ads, in arguments, in headlines. "People who do X get better grades." Maybe X helps. Or maybe the kind of person who does X was already going to do well. Ask what else could explain it before you believe the easy story. This one habit makes you very hard to fool.
