# ⭐ Do Work You're Proud Of

Most students find out what the assignment actually wanted when they get it back with a bad grade. By then it is too late. There is a better order, and it is how real builders work.

## Write the rubric first

You cannot judge your own work if you never set a bar for it. So set it before you start.

Before you write a word, read the prompt and the grading rubric and answer one question: **what does an A actually look like here?** Write it down in your own words. How long, what it has to include, what the teacher is really checking for. Now you are not guessing. You are aiming at something.

Most people skip this and just start typing. Then they drift, run long on the easy part, and miss the thing that was worth half the points. Two minutes with the rubric fixes that.

## Spec before you build. Do not wing it.

Once you know the target, make a quick plan. An outline for an essay. The steps for a project. The list of problems for a set. This is boring and it is the difference between smooth and frantic.

The plan is cheap to change. The finished thing is expensive to change. Move the pieces around while they are still just a bullet list, not at midnight when it is half written.

## AI proposes, you decide, and you are the editor

If you use AI on it, keep the roles straight. It can help you outline, explain a hard part, or check your draft. But you make every real decision, and you catch its mistakes, because it makes them confidently. See [Ask AI the Right Way](Ask%20AI%20the%20Right%20Way.md). The work has to come out of your head and read like you. Anything less and you did not do the assignment, you just held the pen.

## A weak grade is data, not a verdict

Here is a mindset from real work: your first result is a benchmark, not the final answer. A rough draft is supposed to be rough. A low practice-quiz score is not a judgment of you, it is a map of exactly what to study next.

And sometimes a bad result means the foundation was weak, not your effort. If your sources were thin or you did not understand the material yet, no amount of polishing the sentences will save it. Go get better sources, or go actually learn the thing, then build again. You cannot rescue a shaky foundation with fancier work on top.

## The last gate: before you submit

Never turn something in straight off the last sentence you wrote. Run it through the [Before You Submit](../Templates/Before%20You%20Submit.md) checklist first, with your reviewer hat on (see [Be Your Own Reviewer](Be%20Your%20Own%20Reviewer.md)). Does it hit every point in the rubric you wrote at the start? Did you check your own work, not just finish it?

Rubric first. Plan second. Build third. Check last. Do it in that order and you stop being surprised by your grades, because you already know they are good.
