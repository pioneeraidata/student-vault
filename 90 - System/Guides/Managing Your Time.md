# ⏱️ Managing Your Time (When Everything Wants It)

School. A sport. Music. Friends. Sleep. A family that wants to see you. At some point they all want the same Tuesday night, and that is when most people just react, drop something without deciding to, and feel behind all the time. This guide is how you get ahead of it. It is the most grown-up skill in this whole vault, and almost nobody teaches it.

## The one idea: fixed rocks first

Picture a jar. If you fill it with sand first, the big rocks will not fit. Put the big rocks in first, and the sand fills in around them.

Your big rocks are the things that hold a fixed time: school, practice, games, lessons, a job, sleep. They go in first. They live in [[My Commitments]]. Everything flexible, homework, studying, friends, downtime, is the sand. It fills the gaps around the rocks. The mistake is spending a free Tuesday on sand and then having no room for the rock that was coming Thursday.

## The move: plan the week before it happens

Once a week, usually Sunday, spend five minutes with the [Weekly Planner](../Templates/Weekly%20Planner.md). Look at the whole week at once. Drop in the fixed rocks, then block the sand: when will you actually study for Thursday's test, when will you see friends, when do you rest. Seeing all seven days together is how a collision shows up on Sunday instead of ambushing you Wednesday night.

Then each morning, your [daily note](../../01%20-%20Daily/About%20Daily%20Notes.md) turns the plan into today. The weekly planner is the map. The daily note is the next step.

## The truth nobody says out loud: you cannot do everything

Every hour you spend is an hour you did not spend on something else. That is not a motivation problem, it is just math. Trying to do all of it at full effort is how you end up doing all of it badly and exhausted.

So you choose. Some weeks the sport gets the most. Some weeks the test does. Some weeks a friend really needs you and that is the right call. Choosing on purpose is the skill. Every good [Weekly Planner](../Templates/Weekly%20Planner.md) ends with one honest question: **what am I saying no to this week?**

## The danger zone: when rocks stack up

The weeks that wreck people are the ones where big things pile onto the same days. In-season, plus a huge test, plus a recital or a game, plus something social you actually care about. All in one week.

You cannot add hours. So you plan the stack early:

- Spot it on Sunday. A stacked week is obvious a week out if you look.
- Start the studying earlier that week, not the night before, because the night before is already taken.
- Protect sleep anyway. Tired makes everything slower, so cutting sleep to save time usually costs you time.
- Pick the one social thing that matters and let the rest go.
- If something has to give, decide which, on purpose, instead of letting it be whatever you happen to drop.

## Manage energy, not just hours

An hour after a game is not the same as an hour after breakfast. Do the hard, focus-heavy work (math, writing, studying) when your brain is fresh. Save the easy stuff (organizing, light reading, replying to people) for when you are fried. Fighting your own energy is a quiet way to waste hours.

## A few rules that carry a lot

- **Fixed rocks first.** Plan around what cannot move.
- **Name the block.** "Study Tuesday 7 to 8" beats "study more." Vague plans do not happen.
- **Protect sleep and one social thing.** They are not the reward for finishing. They are part of the plan.
- **Say no on purpose.** One clear no protects three yeses that matter.
- **Never miss twice.** Fall off the plan one day, life happens. Just do not let one bad day become the new normal.
